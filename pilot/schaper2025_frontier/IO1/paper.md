A B S T R A C T
Linking scientific articles in PubMed and corporate biomedical U.S. patents, we study the role of inventors who 
are frontier scientists, identified as authors of recent articles in top-general biomedical journals. We find that 
inventions by these “frontier authors” receive more patent citations, are more likely to become technology hits, 
and have broad technology impact. They are also more likely to be internally further developed by the firm, hold 
greater private value, and feature broader claims—not only compared to inventions by non-author inventors but 
also to those by non-frontier authors, including “star” authors. This impact premium is especially strong in 
scaled-up young biopharmaceutical firms and for frontier authors internally employed at the patenting firm. To 
better understand the mechanism behind the impact premium of frontier-author patents, we analyze their 
boundary spanning role. We find that frontier-author patents are more likely to use and to be first users of 
frontier science. However, while frontier-author patents achieve peak impact when referencing frontier science, 
this advantage is comparable to other patents that reference frontier science. And as frontier-author patents also 
enjoy an impact premium on patents referencing other than frontier science, our results, thus, suggest that 
closeness to frontier science in only part of the story of the superior impact of inventions by frontier scientists.
1. Introduction
The economics of innovation literature has since long recognized the 
contribution of new scientific ideas to technological progress in industry 
(Nelson, 1962; Griliches, 1986; Jaffe, 1989; Mansfield, 1995; Cohen 
et al., 2002; Fleming and Sorenson, 2004), yet also made clear that the 
links between science and technology are neither obvious, direct nor 
immediate (among others, (Stephan, 1996; Narin et al., 1997; Ahmad­
poor and Jones, 2017; Iaria et al., 2018)).
More recently, the literature has started to dig deeper into any timing 
advantages for industry science links (e.g., (Arora et al., 2023)), which 
science would matter most for technology development (e.g., (Poege 
et al., 2019; Veugelers and Wang, 2019)) and which individuals can 
effectively translate science into successful inventions. Specifically, 
inventor-authors, who cross the boundaries between scientific research 
and technology development, have been identified as important con­
nectors for establishing industry science links (Gittelman and Kogut, 
2003; Furukawa and Goto, 2006; Bonaccorsi and Thoma, 2007; Breschi 
and Catalini, 2010; Arts and Veugelers, 2020; Herrera, 2020).
Of particular interest is to understand the importance of “frontier 
science”—i.e., the newest science with big and broad impact, shifting 
the frontier of our knowledge—and which firms and inventors would be 
best at linking this frontier science into successful inventions.
In this paper, we examine whether corporate inventors who are at 
the scientific frontier—labelled as “frontier authors” from here on—are 
more impactful. We investigate what distinguishes corporate inventions 
by frontier authors from those of other inventors. We further assess for 
which firm, inventor and technology characteristics a frontier-author 
premium may be particularly pronounced.
To study the relation between frontier authors and impactful in­
ventions we focus on the biomedical science-industry nexus. The 
biomedical industry is a relevant context given the strong connect be­
tween science and technology (e.g., (Gambardella, 1992; Cockburn and 
Henderson, 1998; Henderson et al., 1999; Murray, 2002; Li et al.,

journals in the biomedical discipline, and frontier authors as those in­
ventors with “recent articles in top-general journals”.
We find that firm patents invented by frontier authors have higher 
technology impact (as measured by patent citations received) compared 
to similar patents without authors and, in particular, a much higher 
probability of being hit and broad patents. Importantly, frontier-author 
patents also have significantly higher impact than comparable patents 
by other authors lacking the frontier dimension—i.e., those who pub­
lished in a top-general journal but not recently, those who published 
recently but not in a top-general journal, or those who did neither. These 
impact premia for frontier-author patents hold stronger for first authors 
and decrease substantially when using lower benchmarks for journal or 
article quality, when considering only field-specific top journals, and 
when including less recent publications. These findings are consistent 
with the idea that being active at the scientific frontier—by the recency 
and top-general nature of the science involved—matters particularly for 
fostering technological progress. We further find evidence that the su­
perior performance of frontier-author inventions also holds, if not even 
more strongly—to generate higher private value for their firms. Addi­
tional analyses show that the impact premia for patents by frontier au­
thors are strongest in young, scaled-up biopharmaceutical firms, and 
particularly when frontier authors are internally employed rather than 
when hired externally or when they are external collaborators.
Probing into possible mechanisms driving this pattern, we find that 
patents by frontier authors are not only more likely to cite scientific 
prior art in general—and do so more intensively—but are also signifi­
cantly more likely to cite frontier science, compared to patents by non- 
authors and by authors who are not at the frontier. We further show 
that reliance on frontier science positively relates to more impactful 
inventions, a greater likelihood of technology hits, and higher private 
patent value. However, since this pattern holds for all patents refer­
encing frontier science—not only those by frontier authors—and 
because frontier-author patents outperform even among those without 
frontier science references, their greater use of frontier science can only 
be part of the story of why frontier-scientists' inventions have superior 
impact. Finally, we find no evidence that technology impact premium of 
frontier-author patents depends on being first to cite frontier science. By 
contrast, priority does matter for their private value premium.
Taken together, our results extend the literature on the importance of 
corporate scientists and their impact on innovation in the biomedical 
industry by highlighting the unique contribution of frontier author-in­
ventors compared to other corporate scientists. Corporate patents with 
frontier scientists as inventors are particularly effective in driving 
technological progress in industry, especially for young, scaled-up 
biotech firms. This effect is at least partially driven by these patents' 
ability to build on new scientific insights at the frontier of knowledge. 
Overall, our findings underscore the importance of unpacking the 
frontier nature of science for better understanding the role of inventor- 
authors and prior art references to science in predicting inventions with 
the highest technological impact and private value for firms.
2. A review of the literature and our hypotheses
2.1. Science for technology impact
Scientific advance and technological progress are long known to be 
closely coupled. Using forward citations as an indicator for technology 
impact, Fleming & Sorenson (Fleming and Sorenson, 2004), among 
others, show that patents that rely on scientific prior art have 
significantly more impact on follow-on invention. Scientific inputs have 
also been found to yield inventions with superior private returns 
(Griliches, 1979; Mansfield, 1991; Krieger et al., 2024).
A growing empirical literature has started to investigate which type 
of science is most relevant for technology. Most of these analyses focus 
on assessing the relevance of high quality science, measured as being 
highly cited or published in top cited journals (Poege et al., 2019; Hicks 
et al., 2000).
2.2. Inventor-authors as knowledge brokers between science and 
technological progress in industry
Starting from Arrow (Arrow, 1962), innovation scholars have high­
lighted the existence of widely disconnected communities of scientists 
and inventors. Prior work has stressed the key role of knowledge bro­
kers, i.e., individuals embedded simultaneously in the science and 
technology realms (Allen, 1977; Gruber et al., 2013).
A significant gap in the literature remains regarding our under­
standing of which inventors are best at brokering between science and 
technological progress in industry. Most of the literature in this field 
focuses on “academic scientists”, either in the context of academic 
entrepreneurship (Bercovitz and Feldman, 2008; Grimaldi et al., 2011; 
Bozeman et al., 2013) or in the context of firms collaborating with sci­
entists (e.g., (Cockburn and Henderson, 1998; Fabrizio, 2009)). As 
observed by several studies investigating the birth of the U.S. biotech­
nology industry (e.g., (Zucker and Darby, 1996; Zucker et al., 1998)), 
outcomes of firms collaborating with scientists are highly skewed to­
ward “star” scientists (Zucker et al., 2002; Colen et al., 2022). Other 
studies examine “inventor-authors” on patents (Breschi and Catalini, 
2010; Balconi et al., 2004). Gittelman & Kogut (Gittelman and Kogut, 
2003) find that the number of follow-up citations of patents increases 
with the share of scientific authors among inventors on a patent.
Distinguishing between academic and corporate scientists is impor­
tant due to the different knowledge production regimes in academia and 
industry. However, information on the exact link between the patenting 
firm and the listed inventors is often missing, explaining why less 
research has been conducted on corporate scientists (see Herrera 
(Herrera, 2020) for a survey). Furukawa & Goto (Furukawa and Goto, 
2006), using a sample of five Japanese pharmaceutical companies, show 
that corporate scientists who publish numerous and frequently cited 
papers may not generate more patents themselves, but still create 
“brokerage” by positively influencing the patents of their co-authors. 
Arts & Veugelers (Arts and Veugelers, 2020), on a sample of surveyed 
Ph.D. graduates working in industry in Belgium, show that those who 
engage in academic boundary spanning—as proxied by scientific articles 
co-authored with academic scientists—produce more novel and im­
pactful patents.
2.3. Our hypotheses: frontier authors and their use of frontier science for 
impactful inventions
Both scientific and technological progress are often depicted as 
advancing by a handful of big, new ideas or breakthroughs (Schumpeter, 
1942; Kuhn, 1962; Dosi, 1982; Scherer and Harhoff, 2000; Wang et al., 
2013). This immediately prompts the question of whether new, big ideas 
at the frontier of science are also more relevant for technological prog­
ress, and particularly for new technological breakthroughs. Research 
pushing the scientific frontier provides opportunities for new types of 
inventions, stemming from the translation of a new understanding of 
(new) fundamental principles into impactful technological solutions.
Frontier science is not only characterized by high quality but also by 
its recency and broad impact. The positive relationship between the 
recency of scientific involvement and technology outcomes has received 
some attention in prior studies (e.g., (Gruber et al., 2013; Fabrizio, 
2009)). More recently, Poege et al. (Poege et al., 2019) linked high 
quality and recent science to technology impact by showing that the

Identifying and building on frontier science to create high-impact 
inventions remains particularly challenging. Scientific insights at the 
knowledge frontier are especially difficult to absorb in technological 
invention due to the novelty and implicit nature of embodied ideas and 
methods. This calls for an even more explicit role of inventor-author­
s—knowledge brokers with roots in both communities—who can un­
derstand, analyze and assimilate disparate pieces of knowledge to 
leverage into technology development.
As we expect the scope for impact—and especially big impact—to 
come from the “frontier” of science, defined by the combination of 
excellence, breadth and recency of scientific insights, we single out 
“frontier authors” among inventor-authors on corporate patents. We 
expect these frontier scientists to have an edge not only over non- 
scientists but also over the typically examined “star”/excellent scien­
tists. Indeed, as the frontier of knowledge is rapidly moving (e.g., 
(Mukherjee et al., 2017)), following the obsolescence of their insights, 
any impact advantage of excellent authors in technology development is 
likely to decay with time. Similarly, we compare frontier science to 
domain-specific scientific excellence to isolate the importance of the 
general and broad nature of frontier science impact.
We further explore the mechanisms underlying any inventive per­
formance premium of frontier-author patents. We hypothesize that the 
specific advantage of patents with frontier authors stems from their 
boundary spanning role between science and technology development. 
More specifically, this advantage relates to their enhanced access to the 
rapidly evolving scientific frontier and their ability to use this knowl­
edge as prior art for their inventions—most directly their own frontier 
knowledge, but not exclusively. We expect frontier authors to establish 
links to frontier science rapidly, often even before new scientific 
knowledge becomes public, thereby enjoying significant first-mover 
advantages. Arora et al. (Arora et al., 2023) find that citing scientific 
articles early correlates with higher private patent value, especially for 
articles with strong industrial applicability.
Our main hypotheses can be summarized as follows: 
H1.
: Firm inventions by frontier authors have greater technology 
impact than those by non-frontier authors or non-authors.
H2.
: Firm inventions by frontier authors are more likely and faster to 
use frontier science as prior art than those by non-frontier authors or 
non-authors.
H3.
: Firm inventions by frontier authors that use frontier science as 
prior art have greater technology impact than comparable inventions 
from non-frontier authors or non-authors.
We illustrate our research hypotheses with the case of the invention 
of high-density DNA microarrays. These microarrays have revolution­
ized genomics allowing to analyze the expression levels of thousands of 
genes simultaneously. Beyond ‘inherent’ fields like functional or envi­
ronmental genomics, DNA microarrays are now widely used across 
many areas of medical research, including oncology, pharmacoge­
nomics, or personalized medicine, where they help to predict bio­
markers, assess drug responses, or characterize disease mechanisms. The 
original patent was filed in 1990 by Affymax N.V. (with the division that 
developed it later spun off as Affymetrix Inc.): U.S. Patent No. 5,143,854 
became one of the most impactful patents in the history of the United 
States Patent and Trademark Office, receiving more than 3500 subse­
quent patent citations,1 and Affymetrix—with its landmark product, the 
GeneChip®—the market leader in DNA microarrays.
The inventor team on this patent included neurobiologist Lubert 
Stryer. In the late 1980s, he achieved a major breakthrough by 
discovering the fundamental metabiological mechanism by which 
retinal cells in the human eye perceive light. Two articles on this dis­
covery were published in Science and Nature in 1988 (Meyer et al., 1988; 
Koch and Stryer, 1988). The profound understanding of principles ac­
quired with this scientific breakthrough made Stryer rapidly realize that 
it should conversely be possible to harness the effect of light on mac­
romolecules in cells to synthesize large amounts of compounds in a small 
number of chemical steps—the core idea behind DNA microarrays.
Stryer requested academic leave from Stanford University to 
collaborate with Affymax in pursuing his idea and developing a suitable 
technology. Soon after, the team discovered a process by which they 
succeeded in synthesizing 1024 peptides in only ten steps, as described 
in their influential article published in Science in 1991 (Fodor et al., 
1991). This process was applied to the invention of miniature high- 
density arrays of biological compounds, for which Stryer and col­
leagues began filing a bundle of patents for Affymetrix in 1990—several 
of which became technology hits with broad impact.2 Stryer's seminal 
patent on high-density microarrays of biological compounds cited his 
own recent Science article on this new method, but he and his team also 
leveraged other frontier science for providing a highly successful new 
technological solution.
3. Research setting, data & methods
3.1. Data sources
We analyze the relationship between frontier authors, the use of 
frontier science as prior art, and technology impact for all biomedical 
utility patents assigned to firms and granted by the U.S. Patent and 
Trademark Office (USPTO) between 1980 and 2009. We link this sample 
to the USPTO disambiguated inventor database (Li et al., 2014). Our 
final sample comprises 237,345 patents invented by 159,994 unique 
inventors. We match inventors to authors and scientific non-patent 
references to articles in PubMed/SCI as detailed in the Supplementary 
material.
We further extract the titles and abstracts of all PubMed/SCI articles 
and assess their semantic overlap with the textual descriptions of all U.S. 
patents. Finally, we obtain article-to-article citation data from Microsoft 
Academic Graph (MAG; Sinha et al. (Sinha et al., 2015)) and use 
OpenAlex (Priem et al., 2022) to enrich PubMed author affiliation data. 
In the Supplementary material, we provide further details on data 
processing.
3.2. Measures
3.2.1. Patent impact
We utilize forward citations received to quantify the technology 
impact of patents in our sample. Forward citations are a commonly used 
and established indicator to assess the quality of patents (Trajtenberg, 
1990; Harhoff et al., 1999). For each patent, we count the total number 
of citing patents within the USPTO over a ten-year moving window from 
filing of its worldwide first application (priority date).
To test whether the impact of frontier authors is even more pro­
nounced for breakthroughs, we follow prior literature (e.g., (Ahmadpoor 
and Jones, 2017; Trajtenberg, 1990)) in identifying “hit patents” as 
those entering the top fifth percentile of the citation distribution within 
their primary patent class-year cohort. Similarly, for patents receiving 
forward citations, we additionally measure the breadth (or dispersion) 
of their impact across primary three-digit patent classes using the gen­
erality index from Trajtenberg et al. (Trajtenberg et al., 1997). We 
classify patents in our sample as having broad general impact if they fall

3.2.2. Frontier authors
To identify authors at the frontier of science in our patent sample, we 
leverage the rich heterogeneity of inventors' author footprints in our 
data. We define frontier science based on three dimensions: (1) recency, 
(2) top quality, and (3) broad impact. We classify scientific articles to be 
recent if they were published no more than three years prior to the 
application of a focal patent.4 We consider scientific articles to be of top 
quality and broad impact if they are published in one of the eight most 
prestigious journals in the biomedical disciplines: The New England 
Journal of Medicine, The Lancet, JAMA Journal of the American 
Medical Association, Nature, Science, Cell, Nature Medicine, and 
the BMJ British Medical Journal. Formally, we selected these jour­
nals—which we denote as “top-general journals”—based on the 
following criteria: (1) ranking in the top fifth percentile of the average 
journal impact factor (JIF)5 within a biomedical field and in the nine­
tieth percentile of average JIF across all fields in this category, and (2) 
covering a broad range of fields and topics, i.e., being general-interest. 
We surveyed a group of biomedical researchers to validate our selec­
tion of eight “top-general” biomedical journals. Articles in these journals 
account for a large share of groundbreaking scientific discoveries across 
fields and carry high supra-disciplinary relevance. Subsequently, we 
classify patents as “frontier-author patents” if at least one of their in­
ventors authored a top-general journal article within the three years at 
the time of the first patent application.6 For example, we consider in­
ventor Lubert Stryer a frontier author on the 1990 patent (with priority 
year 1989) covering the invention of DNA micro-arrays, owing to his 
1988 articles in Science (Meyer et al., 1988) and Nature (Koch and Stryer, 
1988). Accordingly, we classify the patent itself as a frontier-author 
patent. “Non-frontier-author patents” are those without frontier au­
thors but with at least one inventor who has authored a non-frontier 
scientific article.
Within the group of non-general (top) journals, we further identify as 
top-field journals, those with a JIF score in the top 5 % of their respective 
biomedical field. This allows us to distinguish non-frontier science into 
top-field science and non-top science. We further split each of these 
categories into recent and non-recent. Together, these distinctions help 
identify which “frontier” characteristics are most decisive for technol­
ogy impact of science: its top-general nature and/or its recency. This 
also enables a contrast with the commonly used “star scientist” concept, 
typically defined by top journal publications that are not necessarily 
recent or general.
3.2.3. Frontier SNPRs
We trace the use of frontier science in inventions by examining the 
scientific prior art references made by patents in the sample.7 Although 
not a perfect measure, SNPRs proxy for a relation between the cited 
scientific idea as prior art input for the development of the citing in­
vention (Tijssen, 2001; Roach and Cohen, 2013; Callaert et al., 2014), 
and are commonly used in the literature.
We rely on the patents-to-PubMed link to differentiate between types 
of SNPRs. Analogous to the definition of frontier authors, we classify 
patents as “frontier-SNPR patents” if at least one of their scientific ref­
erences links to an article in a top-general journal published no more 
than three years before the patent priority-defining application. We 
further define binary indicators for subcategories based on timing (‘first 
to cite”) and on inventors’ proximity to referenced articles (“self-cites”).
As an alternative to relying on SNPRs to detect frontier science links 
in patents, we analyze semantic references to new scientific concepts in 
the summary description of inventions in the text body of patents. 
Similar to Iaria et al. (Iaria et al., 2018), we proxy “frontier-scientific 
terms” by non-common language terms that appear for the first time in 
titles and abstracts of recent scientific articles in top-general journals. 
For details on our text-based approach and the robustness of results, see 
the Supplementary material.
3.3. Sample and summary statistics
Almost 60 % of patents in our sample have at least one inventor with 
prior scientific involvement, confirming the strong connection between 
science and technology in the biomedical field. About 17 % of patents 
are invented by authors with prior top-general journal articles—hence 
would commonly be considered as “star scientists”—but only 7 % fall 
into the category of “frontier-author” patents, i.e., those with at least one 
inventor with recent top-general journal performance.8 The vast majority 
(75 %) of frontier authors do not switch their frontier-author status 
across patenting observations in our sample (Table SM3b–c). Most 
frontier-author patents (72 %) have a single frontier author, who is 
typically also the only “star” scientist on the team (cf. Table SM4a–c). 
These observations underscore the pivotal role of the frontier author in 
these firm inventions.

4.2. Frontier-author patents and their use of frontier SNPRs
In this section, we further zero in on potential mechanisms through 
which the impact premium of frontier-author patents materializes. 
Specifically, we examine whether this advantage is related to a different 
use of scientific knowledge as prior art—particularly frontier scien­
ce—as a mechanism underlying their higher impact (H2–3).
Before examining whether differences in the use of frontier science as 
prior art can explain the impact premium of frontier-author patents, we 
first test whether frontier SNPRs are themselves a potential source of 
higher technology impact. Results reported in the Supplementary ma­
terial (Tables SM12–14; 18) show that patents referencing recent top- 
general science articles receive on average about 35 % more follow-on 
patent citations than patents without any SNPRs—nearly twice the ef­
fect estimated for non-frontier science references. These results are fully 
robust when we measure frontier science links in patents by reuse of 
frontier science terms (Table SM 18). Priority—i.e., being first to cite a 
frontier science article—is not crucial for the technology impact of 
frontier science. The frontier-SNPRs premium also holds for private 
value. However, unlike the results for patent citations, priority does 
translate into higher monetary private value for frontier SNPRs 
(Table SM14, see also (Arora et al., 2023)).
4.2.1. Frontier-author patents and the likelihood of frontier SNPRs
Table 5 reports estimates assessing whether patents by frontier au­
thors are more likely to include frontier SNPRs (H2). Column 1 shows 
that frontier-author patents are significantly more likely to reference 
scientific articles than patents by non-frontier authors or non-authors.24
Column 2 reveals that, among patents citing science, those by frontier 
authors contain considerably more SNPRs on average.25 Such disparity 
in the volume of SNPRs implies that, ceteris paribus, frontier-author 
patents would already stochastically be more likely to include frontier 
SNPRs. To evaluate whether they are also more selective toward frontier 
science in their scientific references, we estimate probabilities condi­
tional on the number of SNPRs (Column 3). The results show that patents 
by frontier authors are considerably more likely to cite frontier science, 
even when holding constant the number of articles cited—more than 
twice as likely as patents without authors. This contrasts markedly with 
patents by non-frontier authors and, thus, supports access to frontier 
science as a potential mechanism underlying the superior performance 
of frontier-author patents.

terms of private value, frontier-author patents also show a significant 
premium even when citing non-self-authored frontier science.
5. Conclusions
In this paper, we contribute to the literature on industry-science links 
by identifying and illustrating the advantage for firms in the biomedical 
industry of having scientists active at the scientific frontier as inventors 
on their patents. Unlike the commonly studied highly cited scientists, 
frontier scientists produce science that is not only excellent but also recent 
and broadly impactful. We show that corporate inventions developed by 
frontier scientists achieve significantly higher and broader technology 
impact, exceeding that of inventions by non-authors as well as by top 
authors further away from the frontier, whether in time or general 
quality. This impact premium is particularly pronounced in scaled-up 
young firms and when frontier scientists are closely connected as in­
ternal employees of the patenting firm.
We further show that the citation premium of frontier-author patents 
is partially driven by their ability to identify and build on new scientific 
insights at the frontier of knowledge, as reflected in their greater incli­
nation and intensity to use science as prior art—particularly the use of 
frontier science, including but not limited to their own. Although fron­
tier authors possess a first mover advantage in accessing frontier science, 
we find no evidence that this advantage translates into higher technol­
ogy impact. Moreover, since the impact premium of frontier-author 
patents also holds for inventions without links to science, our results 
suggest that their superior performance is a bigger story than access to 
frontier science alone.
Our main results are robust to several alternative approaches, 
including the use of text-mining techniques rather than scientific ref­
erences (SNPRs) to identify reliance on new scientific ideas in corporate 
patents. When examining the private value of patents rather than cita­
tion impact, the superior performance of frontier-author patents and 
frontier-SNPR patents is not only confirmed but appears even more 
pronounced. Frontier-author patents are also significantly more likely to 
be used internally by firms as prior art for subsequent technology de­
velopments. For private value, access to frontier science—including 
priority access—provides a significantly higher premium for inventions 
by frontier authors, even when citing non-self-authored frontier science. 
Yet, much like the technology impact premium, the superior private 
value of frontier-author inventions cannot be explained solely by pri­
ority access to frontier science.
Our findings primarily contribute to the literature on the returns 
from science for firms in technology development (Gittelman and Kogut, 
2003; Bonaccorsi and Thoma, 2007; Gruber et al., 2013). We emphasize 
the importance of the frontier nature of both the inventor-author link and 
the scientific prior art used—distinguishing it from other types of sci­
ence, including top-field science. We show that frontier inventor-authors 
act as distinctive “bridge-builders” between science and technology 
(Breschi and Catalini, 2010). The superior inventive performance of 
frontier-author patents, compared to those of other inventor-authors, is 
at least partly driven by their ability to use frontier science, inclu­
ding—but not limited to— their ownfrontier science.
Our findings also contribute to the literature on firm participation in 
scientific research (Gambardella, 1992; Rosenberg, 1990; Arora et al., 
2021). We highlight the heterogeneity of firm environments that are 
most conducive to realizing a frontier-author patent impact premium. 
Young, scaled-up firms—typically in biotechnology—appear best posi­
tioned to benefit from frontier inventor-authors, with the premium 
being significantly larger when the frontier author is an internal 
employee of the patenting firm.
Our findings also speak to recent work on channels of early access for 
firms to new scientific findings (Arora et al., 2023; Baruffaldi and Poege, 
2025). Early access to frontier science pays off, but only in terms of 
private value, not citation impact. While frontier-author patents do 
benefit from early access to frontier science—including beyond their 
own—they also exhibit an impact premium without first access, sug­
gesting that priority to frontier science is only part of the explanation for 
the superior performance of frontier-author inventions.
Overall, our findings strongly support the view that involving in­
ventors active at the frontier of science enables firms to generate com­
mercial inventions with superior technology and higher private value.
