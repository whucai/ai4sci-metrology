# Enhancing the robustness of the disruption metric against noise

Nan Deng1 · An Zeng1

Received: 6 July 2022 / Accepted: 21 January 2023 / Published online: 11 February 2023   
© Akadémiai Kiadó, Budapest, Hungary 2023

# Abstract

Measuring the novelty of scientific papers is an important research topic. If most subsequent researches of a focal paper only cite itself instead of citing its references as well, this paper could be highly disruptive as it may start a new stream of research. However, due to preferential attachment, even if a focal paper is very disruptive, the subsequent works may still cite both the focal paper and some of its highly cited references. To eliminate the noise caused by these highly cited references, we modify the disruption metric and analyze its performance and robustness. The results show that the improved method could better distinguish Nobel prize winning papers from the others. In addition, the resultant ranking is more stable against the highly cited references and random link removal on citation network.

Keywords  Citation networks · Scientific novelty · Bibliometrics · Disruption index

# Introduction

Novel or disruptive innovations and researches prompt  advances in science (Zeng et al., 2017). What kind of mechanism may lead to creative research has been studied extensively from the perspectives of psychology and sociology, but the quantitative research started in recent years.

Novelty may lead to creative achievements. For technology, new inventions are thought to consist of several existing technologies (Fleming et al., 2007). The combinatorial process of technologies was formally and quantitatively characterized by Youn et al. (2014) and was proved to show consistent rates of exploitation and exploration. For science, this combinatorial process could be embodied in the scientific papers’ citing behaviors. A creative paper may cite previous works in different research fields, which combined existing knowledge. The highest-impact works balance the atypical and traditional combinations well (Uzzi et al., 2013). A method was proposed to identify the recombination of previous works based on co-citation networks (Mukherjee et al., 2016). Creative idea comes from interdisciplinary exchange (Tahamtan & Bornmann, 2018). It is also found that papers whose references with lower mean age but higher age variance are probably getting more citations (Mukherjee et  al., 2017). In general, projects with higher novelty don’t receive funds with higher possibility. Projects with intermediate levels of novelty are more popular with funding organizations(Criscuolo et al., 2017). The greater the degree of interdisciplinarity, the lower the probability of being funded (Bromham et al., 2016). A survey on the most-cited authors of biomedical fields showed that scientists thought their top works were evolutionary rather than revolutionary (Ioannidis et al., 2014).

The  disruption metric was newly introduced to measure creativity. This metric was brought to measure how a new invention consolidates or destabilizes existing technologies (Funk & Owen-Smith, 2017). Then, it was applied to science. Research revealed that large teams tended to develop science while small teams were more likely to disrupt science and technology (Wu et  al., 2019).  The disruption metric has limitations. For instance, highly cited references of focal papers make the disruption more close to zero (Wu &Wu, 2019). Bornmann verified this phenomenon by discussing papers published in Scientometrics and asserted that disruption was more suitable to differentiate papers at the very ends of the disruption and the index was affected by authors’ citing behavior (Bornmann et al., 2020a, b). Disruption got stable after publishing for several years, so an appropriate citation window was suggested (Bornmann et  al., 2020a, b). By analyzing different indicators based on the three types of citations proposed by Wu et  al. only when the focal paper and its references are relatively close to the same topics, disruption indicators containing nk(will be explained in Section “Method”) can be effective (Wu &Yan, 2019). Bu et  al. (2019) proposed an indicator measuring the independence of citation impact, which was related to disruption proposed by Wu et al. (2019) by removing nk from the formula. Comparing some variant indicators of disruption, one of them is slightly better than the others. This variant sets a threshold for the  focal paper’s references—having at least 5 bibliographic coupling links with the focal paper (Bornmann et al., 2020a, b)

Now existing subsequent researches on improving the disruption metric mainly focused on two perspectives—one is to find a variant of the original metric and the other is to set some thresholds and references failing to meet these thresholds will be removed from the citation network of the focal paper. In this article, we improve the disruption metric by eliminating the impact of highly cited references of the focal paper. Instead of deleting nodes representing highly cited papers from the citation network, our method is to remove edges directed to those highly cited papers, which could retain all the node information and meanwhile reduce the impact of highly cited papers. Compared with the original disruption metric, our modified index could better distinguish innovative papers from developing papers and has stronger robustness against random link removal.

# Data set and method

# Data set

We analyzed the publication data provided by American Physical Society (APS). The author name data after a comprehensive disambiguation process is provided by Sinatra et al. (2016). This data set concludes 482,566 papers published from 1893 to 2010.

To test the performance of the modified disruption metric, we focused on how did it improve the average rank of the highly innovative works (as the benchmark data). We pick out 87 Milestone Letters, 49 Nobel prize winning papers as the benchmark data. In 2008, Physics Review Letters (PRL) compiled a series called Milestone Letters, starting in 1958 and continuing through 2000. Papers in this series are thought to make important development in physics or start a brand new field. Nobel papers are those winning Nobel Prize in this data set. In addition, we pick some highly developmental works such as review articles published in Review of Modern Physics. Review articles summarize and comment on the previous works of a specific field, which should be less disruptive. We select 1671 reviews published in Review of Modern Physics, with at least 5 citations.

# Method

Disruption is a metric proposed by Funk et al. in 2017 and applied in science by Wu et al.   
in 2019, which is demonstrated in Fig. 1. Suppose that a focal paper(blue square) has sev  
eral references(circle). Diamonds refer to the papers published after the focal paper. Green   
ones may only cite the focal paper, purple ones cite both the focal paper and its references,   
while black ones cite its references only. Variables, ni, nj, nk are re−spectively numbers of ni nj   
green, purple and black diamonds. And we have: disruption = ni + nj + nk

Impactful papers can be cited repeatedly in one field, because the point of view brought by it has become academic consensus. This phenomenon could cause noise in the calculation of disruption. We call the highly cited important papers hot-spot papers. If a focal paper cites one hot-spot paper(red circle) and some of its citing papers also cite the hot-spot paper, the disruption index may be underestimated. And as the citing papers are published later than the focal paper, the influence from the hot-spot papers may become weaker due to the longer time interval.

To eliminate the effect of hot-spot papers and differentiate the citations respectively   
from the focal paper and from its citing papers, we cut edges from the purple diamonds   
to the red circle, eliminating hot-spot works’ contribution to the citing papers. In other   
words, we turn some purple diamonds into green ones. As Fig. 1 illustrates, when we   
reshape the network structure, nj decreases, ni and disruption increase. Now we have ni − nj   
new disruption = ni + nj + nk

![](images/a57134f561d7e9c4baad835d7ed456471980f0a849c28353ff94ccf0554a6f62.jpg)  
Fig. 1   Illustration of disruption proposed by Funk et al. (2017) and our modified disruption metric

Hot-spot papers are top highly cited papers with a threshold indicating how much the hot-spot papers account for. For example, if we set the threshold to 1% , it means that we select the top 1% highest cited papers as hot-spot papers. The example shown in Fig. 1 indicates that cutting the edges could increase the disruption value of the focal paper.

It is widely agreed that papers directly leading to Nobel prizes tended to exhibit high levels of disruptiveness, whereas, review articles tended to consolidate their fields, and thus have small disruption values (Azoulay et al., 2018). That means if the new disruption makes Nobel winning papers rank higher while review papers rank lower, the effectiveness of the new method is validated.

Accordingly, we rank 230,867 papers with 5 or more citations out of all the 482,566 papers, according to the disruption and DI5 brought by Bornmann et al.. Then we calculate the new disruption of each paper when eliminating different fraction of hot-spot papers starting from the most cited papers. And for the list ranked by the new disruption we call it the new rank. Figure 2 shows that Kendall’s 휏 between the original and the new ranks decreases with the percentile (i.e. the fraction of hot-spot papers eliminated). The results suggest that excluding the effect of hot-spot papers would significantly change the evaluation of a paper’s disruption.

# Results

Then we vary the eliminated fraction of hot-spot papers and compute the change of Nobel, Milestone and Review papers’ average ranks. The percentile ranges from zero to 20%. When it is equivalent to zero, there is no hot-spot papers eliminated and the new disruption is the same as the original disruption. Larger percentile means more hot-spot papers are eliminated and papers’ disruption index will change more significantly.

Our result shows that the best percentile of hot-spot papers is 3%. On one hand, the new disruption improves highly innovative works’ ranking. On the other hand, it suppresses Review papers’ ranking. We compare the original disruption ranks with the new ranks of Nobel, Milestone and Review papers, when we remove 3% hot-spot papers. We calculate the average disruption values of 3 categories at first, then locate the ranks of the mean values. Nobel papers’ rank rises from 10,812 to 8,763 and Milestone papers’ rank rises from 12,661 to 8,793. Reviews’ rank drops from 19,647 to 23,058. In fig.4a, the percentile rank of Nobel is improved from 95.32 to 96.20, the percentile rank of Milestone is improved from 94.53 to 96.19, and the percentile rank of Review papers drops from 92.74 to 90.01. The variations are shown in the inset. We draw scatter plots to see if the improvements are general in most papers instead of being caused by the huge variation in a few papers. And we compare the new disruption with the original one and DI5, by computing 3 disruption indexes of Nobel, Milestone and Review papers(Fig.  4b,c,d). Generally, our new disruption and DI5 both could improve Nobel and Milestone papers’ ranking while suppress Review papers’ ranking. Our method makes 61.2% Nobel papers(30 put of 49) and 77.0% Milestone papers(67 put of 87) rank higher, and 30.8% Review letters(514 put of 1671) rank lower. The results of T-test indicate that our method’s improvement is statistically significant compared to the original disruption and DI5. But when it comes to developmental review articles, DI5’s performance is significantly better than the others. DI5 rises   the  ranks of 46.9% Nobel papers(23 out of 49) and 58.6% Milestone papers(51 put of 87), while suppresses the ranks of 23.1% Review papers(386 out of 1671).

To better understand the variation of ranks, we trace the top 40000 highly disruptive papers, ranked by the original disruption metric. Then we compare their original ranks with the new ranks. We illustrate two situations with different percentiles, namely 3%(the best) and 10%. Setting higher percentile of hot-spot papers changes the ranking distribution more significantly (Fig. 5a, c). Panel (c) with 10% hot-spot papers presents a wider rank distribution than panel (a) with 3% hot-spot papers. This indicates that if we eliminate more hot-spot papers, more top papers’ ranks may drop. We also notice that the frequency of papers in the top ranking exhibits no change. Then we draw a scatter plot to find out the detailed relation between the original and the new ranks (Fig. 5b, d). The figures illustrate that the ranks of top disruptive papers change little, and in the scatter plot it is shown as a straight line. This is because disruptions of top ranked papers are equal to 1, and if their references don’t include hot-spot papers, ni and nj will not change, so their new disruptions are still 1. The straight line interrupts at one point, from which massive papers’ ranks change. We call it a turning point. The turning point depends on how many top papers’ disruption values remain unchanged. Setting higher fraction of hot-spot papers leads to more papers’ ranks changing, but it doesn’t change the turning point obviously (Fig.  5b, d), which means that our new method may not influence papers with disruption values of 1.

The new disruption supports the conclusion that disruption decreases with team size (Wu et al., 2019). Figure 6 shows the variance of disruption with team size. We set 3% and 10% highly cited papers as hot-spot ones and reshape the citation networks when computing the new disruption. We then calculate the average new disruptions of papers with different team sizes, and compare them with the original disruption. The three situations appear to have similar declining tendency. Small teams are more probably to produce disruptive works. The declining trend slows down when the team size is over 3.

Both the data missing and the malicious citing behavior may have effects on the disruption metric. To test the metric’s ability to overcome the noises in citation data, we study the robustness of papers’ ranking according to the new disruption. For this purpose, we remove the edges from the citation network randomly and compare the change of disruption ranks (Fig.  7). We adopt Kendall’s 휏 between ranks before and after link removal to quantify the robustness. Figure 7 shows that for both link removal methods(reshuffling and cutting edges), new disruption ranks have higher correlation coefficient between the ranks before and after link removing, indicating that the new disruption metric has stronger robustness than the original disruption. DI5 also has stronger robustness than the original disruption. And it has roughly the same performance as the new disruption when reshuffling edges while performs not as well as the new disruption when cutting edges.

# Discussion

Quantifying scientific achievements is an important and still-developing topic. It is not easy to quantify and compare the creativity of research papers. To depict a paper, one could measure multiple dimensions, such as impact, novelty and interdisciplinarity. Disruptive works may provide us with brand new scientific visions, and identifying those papers with high disruption and originality may help us solve new problems and develop new academic fields. By optimizing the disruption quantitative index, we can improve our now-existing judge and rewarding systems.

Disruption is an index measuring the creativity of patents or research papers based on the citation network of the focal paper. The original disruption is defined by the difference between the proportion of papers citing the focal paper only and papers citing both the focal paper and its references. To improve the disruption metric, we modify the original one by eliminating the impact of highly cited references. In other words, we reshape the citation network of the focal paper by cutting edges from its citing papers directed to hot-spot papers. There might be some risk of deleting citations between a focal paper and hot-spot papers very similar in content. However, this micro side-effect may only influence the use of disruption metric for evaluating individual papers. In fact, the metric disruption itself is only statistically useful. The disruption cannot be used for identifying individual papers with high originality. This is because the disruption metric is a fraction which could be extremely large if a paper has a small denominator (very few citations). If one aims to select the most original paper with the disruption metric, he/she would most likely get a paper with very few citations (the disruption is high if these one or two citations happen to miss the references of the paper). In most studies, the disruption metric is used for measuring the originality of a group of papers. For instance, in Wu’s article (Wu et al., 2019) the authors only use the metric to compare the group of papers with few authors and the group of papers with many authors. As the disruption metric is designed for the purpose of statistical use, our modification will still be useful.

Our analysis shows that the new disruption could tell Nobel prize winning papers apart. We analyze the disruption rank of Nobel, Milestone and review papers. The Nobel prize winning papers and Milestone Letters are ranked the highest when removing edges from citing papers to the top 3% highly cited references. Using the new disruption metric, we can improve the innovative papers’ ranks. Meanwhile, review articles are ranked lower than before.

We also show that the new disruption has stronger robustness, namely the ability to resist random inaccurate data. Less change of rank means stronger robustness. If we adopt the modified disruption metric, we will observe that the ranks change less under two scenarios, namely random edge reshuffling and random edge cutting.

The future extensions could focus on solving the problem that the disruption values are concentrated near zero, because of the large nk caused by highly cited papers, which is not addressed in our article. One can also further improve the metric by eliminating some unreliable citations. For example, the citation from a low-impact paper may be not as important as that from a high-impact paper, or sometimes bring even noisy information for evaluating the  originality of the focal paper. In future work, differences between citations could be considered by weighting citations from papers of different impacts or directly removing citations from low impact papers. Moreover, when quantifying the significance of papers, one can adopt PageRank algorithm or the variants of citation, for example C10(i.e. the number of citations 10 years after a paper is published), to measure the impact of publications.
